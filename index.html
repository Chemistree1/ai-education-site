AI in Education: Regulation Over Restriction

Artificial intelligence is reshaping how students learn, write, research, and solve problems. The central question is not whether AI belongs in education, but how schools can govern its use without weakening authentic learning.

Read the Research View Works Cited

Overview of the Issue

AI tools can help students brainstorm, summarize, and revise; however, they can also be used to circumvent the intellectual processes that assignments are designed to develop. When students rely on generated responses instead of constructing their own ideas, schools face new questions about academic integrity, fairness, and authentic learning.

Research on AI in education identifies several interconnected concerns, including moral dissonance in student work, uncertainty among teachers, inconsistent policy guidance, and the need for AI literacy. Taken together, these concerns suggest that regulation is more effective than simple restriction.

Why AI Matters in Education

AI matters because students will continue to encounter and use these tools both in school and beyond it. Classrooms cannot ignore a technology that already shapes writing, research, and communication. Instead, education must teach students how to use AI responsibly while preserving the value of independent thought.

When integrated thoughtfully, AI can support tutoring, organization, and accessibility. When used without clear boundaries, it can weaken critical thinking and make it more difficult for teachers to determine what students genuinely understand.

Core Argument

Regulation over restriction

Schools should not respond to AI with blanket bans. A stronger approach is to regulate how AI is used through classroom expectations, disclosure practices, teacher support, and AI literacy instruction. Regulation protects academic integrity while still preparing students for a future in which AI will remain part of learning and professional life.

Moral Dissonance

Caling demonstrates that AI-generated academic work can create a conflict between submitted performance and genuine student effort.

Teacher Uncertainty

Alvarez highlights how teachers are being asked to adapt in real time, often without clear institutional guidance.

Policy and Literacy

Malkus and Riegel point toward long-term solutions grounded in policy development and AI literacy.
