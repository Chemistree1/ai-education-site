Research and Analysis

This page develops the argument that education needs regulation, not restriction, in response to AI. The analysis focuses on misuse, the erosion of critical thinking, teacher uncertainty, moral dissonance, policy urgency, and AI literacy as a long-term solution.

1. The Problem

AI misuse in education

AI tools can support learning, but they can also be used to bypass it. Students may use generative AI to draft essays, answer questions, or produce analysis without engaging the reasoning processes that education is intended to cultivate.

This creates a serious problem for schools. If students submit polished work that they did not meaningfully develop themselves, grades no longer reflect genuine understanding. The classroom becomes less concerned with learning and more concerned with managing output.

Students bypass learning processes when they rely on AI to brainstorm, organize, explain, or write in place of their own effort. Such reliance weakens the purpose of assignments designed to build skills through sustained practice.

Core problem

The danger is not merely that AI can help students finish work more quickly. It is that students can appear successful without completing the intellectual work that learning requires.

2. Impact

What this changes for students and teachers

Loss of critical thinking

When AI assumes responsibility for explanation and analysis, students lose repeated practice with judgment, reasoning, and original thought.

Assessment difficulty

Teachers face greater difficulty determining whether submitted work reflects student understanding or automated assistance.

Teacher uncertainty

Alvarez shows that teachers are adapting quickly, often without clear rules governing acceptable AI use, assignment redesign, or accountability.

The impact is both academic and structural. Students may become less confident in their own thinking, while teachers are left attempting to evaluate student work within a system that has not yet clearly defined the role of AI.

3. Key Concepts

Ideas shaping the debate

Moral dissonance

Caling argues that AI-generated academic work can create moral dissonance when students receive credit for ideas and effort that are not fully their own.

Teacher adaptation

Alvarez emphasizes that teachers must adapt their methods, expectations, and assessments as AI becomes more common in classroom practice.

Policy urgency

Malkus shows that schools need clear policy sooner rather than later because inconsistent responses create confusion rather than accountability.

4. Solutions

Short-term and long-term responses

Short-term classroom strategies

  • Require process work such as outlines, drafts, and reflections.
  • Use in-class writing and discussion to confirm student understanding.
  • Set explicit rules for when AI use must be disclosed.
  • Design assignments that ask for personal reasoning and revision choices.

Long-term policy solutions

  • Develop schoolwide AI policy instead of leaving every teacher to improvise.
  • Provide teacher training so educators can adapt with confidence.
  • Integrate AI literacy into curriculum, following Riegel's emphasis on informed use.
  • Create standards that balance innovation with academic integrity.

5. Future of Education

Preparing for AI integration

AI integration will continue to expand across education. Students will use these tools in academic, workplace, and everyday contexts, which means schools cannot rely on avoidance as a long-term strategy.

Proactive policy matters because delay creates larger problems later. If schools postpone action, teacher uncertainty increases, student expectations drift, and assessment becomes less reliable.

The future of education depends on building systems that help students use AI without surrendering the reasoning, reflection, and intellectual honesty that education is meant to strengthen.

Why proactive policy matters

The schools that are best prepared for AI will not be the ones that ban it most aggressively. Rather, they will be the ones that regulate it clearly, train teachers well, and teach students how to use it responsibly.
